幽霊 · Yūrei · Ghosts

Haunted

Places where the dead are said to linger. Tread carefully.

Yamanashi Prefecture · Haunted

Aokigahara

青木ヶ原 — The Sea of Trees
📍 Northwest flank of Mount Fuji, Yamanashi

A 35 km² forest growing on ancient hardened lava from Fuji's last major eruption in 864 CE. The porous lava rock absorbs all sound. Step off the trail and silence swallows you whole.

Known for centuries as a dwelling place of yūrei — restless spirits from Japanese mythology. The forest is so dense that compasses reportedly malfunction due to magnetic iron in the volcanic soil.

Visiting

Stick to marked trails. The ice caves on the western edge are the main tourist draw. Go early morning when the mist sits low — unforgettable atmosphere.

Fukuoka Prefecture · Haunted

Inunaki Village

犬鳴村 — The Howling Village
📍 Inunaki Mountain Pass, Fukuoka

Japan's most famous urban legend. A village that supposedly rejected the Japanese constitution, where a sign reads: "The laws of Japan do not apply beyond this point." Visitors who enter are said to never return.

The real Inunaki Village existed from 1691–1889 and was flooded by a dam. The Old Inunaki Tunnel nearby is now sealed but remains one of Japan's most notorious "haunted" spots.

Visiting

The old tunnel is sealed. The area around Inunaki Dam is accessible and genuinely eerie. Best visited during daylight. Don't go alone at night — it's remote and poorly lit.

Nagasaki Prefecture · Abandoned

Hashima Island

端島 — Battleship Island (Gunkanjima)
📍 15 km off Nagasaki coast

A concrete ghost island that once held the highest population density on Earth — 5,259 people crammed onto 6.3 hectares in 1959. Built for undersea coal mining, it had apartments, a school, hospital, cinema, and even a rooftop garden.

When the coal ran out in 1974, everyone left overnight. You might recognize it as the villain's lair in the James Bond film Skyfall.

Visiting

UNESCO World Heritage Site. Boat tours from Nagasaki Port (~¥4,000–5,000). Tours cancel in rough weather. Book in advance — they sell out.

Nara Prefecture · Abandoned

Nara Dreamland

奈良ドリームランド — Japan's Dead Disneyland
📍 Nara City

A Disneyland knockoff that opened in 1961. Complete with a Sleeping Beauty Castle replica, six roller coasters, and its own Main Street.

When the real Tokyo Disneyland opened in 1983, attendance collapsed. It limped along until 2006, then was abandoned entirely — left to rot with rides still standing, mascot statues gathering moss. It became the holy grail of urban explorers before demolition in 2016–2017.

Note

⚠️ Demolished in 2016–2017. The site is now cleared. This entry is here for history — it was one of the most photographed abandoned places in Japan.

Saitama Prefecture · Abandoned

Nichitsu Ghost Town

ニッチツ鉱山 — The Mountain Ghost Town
📍 Chichibu, Saitama — 3 hours from Tokyo

Deep in the mountains of Saitama, an entire mining town sits abandoned. Houses, a hospital, a school — all frozen in time. Textbooks still sit on desks. Personal belongings litter the floors.

It's like the residents just walked out one day and never came back. The surrounding mountain scenery adds to the otherworldly feeling.

Visiting

Technically private property — enter at your own risk and be respectful. Accessible by car via mountain roads from Chichibu. Some buildings are structurally unsound.

暗い歴史 · Dark History

Dark History

Beautiful places with terrible pasts.

Hiroshima Prefecture · Dark History

Ōkunoshima

大久野島 — The Poison Gas Island (Rabbit Island)
📍 Seto Inland Sea, Hiroshima

Today it's known as "Rabbit Island" — hundreds of friendly wild rabbits hop up to visitors begging for food. Adorable. But the cute exterior hides one of Japan's darkest secrets.

From 1929 to 1945, this island was a secret chemical weapons factory producing poison gas for Japan's war in China. It was literally erased from maps. The ruined gas factory still stands, crumbling among the rabbits.

Visiting

Ferry from Tadanoumi Port (~15 min). Visit the Poison Gas Museum (¥100). Bring rabbit food from the mainland. The contrast between cute rabbits and war ruins is surreal.

Iwate Prefecture · Dark History

Matsuo Mine Apartments

松尾鉱山 — The Ruins in the Clouds
📍 Hachimantai, Iwate

At 1,000 meters elevation, massive concrete apartment blocks emerge from the fog like something from a post-apocalyptic film. Once home to 15,000 miners and their families, this sulfur mining town was called "a paradise above the clouds."

When the mine closed in 1969, the entire community vanished. In fog, the buildings appear and disappear like ghosts.

Visiting

Visible from the road to Hachimantai. Buildings are fenced off — view from outside. Best combined with a trip to Hachimantai hot springs. Autumn foliage makes the contrast stunning.

Supernatural

Where the living world meets the dead. Japan takes this literally.

Aomori Prefecture · Supernatural

Mount Osore

恐山 — The Mountain of Dread
📍 Shimokita Peninsula, Aomori — northernmost Honshu

One of Japan's three most sacred places — and a literal gateway to the afterlife. A Buddhist temple sits inside the caldera of an active volcano. Sulfurous steam rises from barren yellow earth, the stench of rotten eggs fills the air, and a pristine turquoise lake sits in the middle of it all.

The name means "Mountain of Dread." Founded in 862 CE. Blind spirit mediums called itako gather here during summer festivals to channel the dead.

Visiting

Open May–October only. Entry ¥500. The Bodai-ji temple has free onsen baths fed by volcanic springs. The Itako Taisai festival (July) is when mediums communicate with the dead. Remote but worth the journey.

Iwate Prefecture · Supernatural

Tōno Valley

遠野 — Where Folklore Is Real
📍 Tōno City, Iwate

A rural valley where Japanese folklore isn't stories — it's daily life. This is the home of Tōno Monogatari (1910), the most important collection of Japanese folk tales ever published. The locals still leave cucumbers at the river for kappa — water demons.

The valley is dotted with shrines to oshira-sama, zashiki-warashi (child ghosts that haunt houses and bring good fortune), and the kappa pool. This is genuine, living folklore — not played up for tourists.

Visiting

Rent a bicycle and explore the valley. Visit Kappa-buchi pool, the Tōno Folklore Museum, and Denshōen garden. Stay at a traditional farmhouse (magariya). 2–3 hours from Morioka by train.

Before You Go

⚠️

Respect the Sites

Many of these places are sacred, dangerous, or both. Don't be the tourist who takes selfies at a memorial.

🗣️

Language Barrier

These aren't tourist hotspots. English signage is rare. Learn basic Japanese or use Google Translate's camera mode.

🚗

You'll Need a Car

Most of these places are off the train grid. Rent a car — Japanese roads are excellent. Get an International Driving Permit.

📱

Pocket WiFi

Cell signal is spotty in mountain areas. Rent a pocket WiFi router at the airport. Download offline maps for remote areas.

🏚️

Haikyo Etiquette

Don't break in. Don't take souvenirs. Don't post hidden locations publicly. Take only photos, leave only footprints.

🌧️

Weather Matters

These places are 10x more atmospheric in fog or rain. Mt. Osore is only open May–October. Matsuo Mine is best in autumn.
