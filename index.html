幽霊 · Yūrei

Haunted

Places where the dead are said to linger. Tread carefully.

Yamanashi Prefecture

Aokigahara

青木ヶ原 — The Sea of Trees
📍 Northwest flank of Mount Fuji, Yamanashi

A 35 km² forest growing on ancient hardened lava from Fuji's last major eruption in 864 CE. The porous lava rock absorbs all sound. Step off the trail and silence swallows you whole.

Known for centuries as a dwelling place of yūrei — restless spirits from Japanese mythology. The forest is so dense that compasses reportedly malfunction due to magnetic iron in the volcanic soil. Since the 1960s, it gained a much darker reputation that made it infamous worldwide.

Visiting

Stick to marked trails. The ice caves on the western edge are the main tourist draw. Guided nature walks available. Go early morning when the mist sits low — unforgettable atmosphere.

Fukuoka Prefecture

Inunaki Village & Old Inunaki Tunnel

犬鳴村 — The Howling Village
📍 Inunaki Mountain Pass, Fukuoka

Japan's most famous urban legend. A village that supposedly rejected the Japanese constitution, where a sign reads: "The laws of Japan do not apply beyond this point." Visitors who enter are said to never return.

The real Inunaki Village existed from 1691-1889 and was flooded by a dam. The Old Inunaki Tunnel nearby is now sealed but remains one of Japan's most notorious "haunted" spots. The legend inspired a 2019 horror film.

Visiting

The old tunnel is sealed. The area around Inunaki Dam and the mountain pass is accessible and genuinely eerie. Best visited during daylight. Don't go alone at night — not because of ghosts, but because it's remote and poorly lit.

Nagasaki Prefecture

Hashima Island (Gunkanjima)

端島 — Battleship Island
📍 15 km off Nagasaki coast

A concrete ghost island that once held the highest population density on Earth — 5,259 people crammed onto 6.3 hectares in 1959. Built for undersea coal mining, it had apartment blocks, a school, hospital, cinema, and even a rooftop garden. All on a rock in the sea.

When the coal ran out in 1974, everyone left. The entire island was abandoned overnight. Now its brutalist concrete buildings crumble into the Pacific, untouched for decades. You might recognize it as the villain's lair in the James Bond film Skyfall.

Visiting

UNESCO World Heritage Site. Boat tours depart from Nagasaki Port (~¥4,000-5,000). You can walk a designated path on the island. Tours cancel in rough weather. Book in advance — they sell out.

Nara Prefecture

Nara Dreamland

奈良ドリームランド — Japan's Dead Disneyland
📍 Nara City, Nara

A Disneyland knockoff that opened in 1961 after a Japanese businessman visited the original Anaheim park and wanted to build his own. Complete with a Sleeping Beauty Castle replica, six roller coasters, and its own Main Street.

When the real Tokyo Disneyland opened in 1983, attendance collapsed. It limped along until 2006, then was abandoned entirely — left to rot with rides still standing, mascot statues gathering moss. It became the holy grail of urban explorers before demolition in 2016-2017.

Visiting

⚠️ Demolished. The park was torn down in 2016-2017. The site is now cleared. This entry is here for history — it was one of the most photographed abandoned places in Japan. The photos and videos that remain online are haunting.

Saitama Prefecture

Nichitsu Ghost Town

ニッチツ鉱山 — The Mountain Ghost Town
📍 Chichibu, Saitama (3 hours from Tokyo)

Deep in the mountains of Saitama, an entire mining town sits abandoned. Houses, a hospital, a school — all frozen in time since the mine closed. Textbooks still sit on desks. Personal belongings litter the floors.

What makes Nichitsu unique is its accessibility from Tokyo and the sheer completeness of what was left behind. It's like the residents just... walked out one day and never came back. The surrounding mountain scenery adds to the otherworldly feeling.

Visiting

The area is technically private property — enter at your own risk and be respectful. Accessible by car via mountain roads from Chichibu. The drive itself through the gorge is spectacular. Some buildings are structurally unsound.

暗い歴史 · Dark History

Dark History

Beautiful places with terrible pasts. History doesn't always come gift-wrapped.

Hiroshima Prefecture

Ōkunoshima (Rabbit Island)

大久野島 — The Poison Gas Island
📍 Seto Inland Sea, Hiroshima Prefecture

Today it's known as "Rabbit Island" — hundreds of friendly wild rabbits hop up to visitors begging for food. Instagram gold. Adorable. But the cute exterior hides one of Japan's darkest secrets.

From 1929 to 1945, this island was a secret chemical weapons factory producing poison gas for Japan's war in China. It was literally erased from maps. Workers weren't told what they were making. The ruined gas factory and power plant still stand, crumbling among the rabbits.

Visiting

Ferry from Tadanoumi Port (~15 min). There's a resort hotel and camping. Visit the Poison Gas Museum (¥100) — small but impactful. Bring rabbit food from the mainland. The contrast between cute rabbits and war ruins is surreal.

Iwate Prefecture

Matsuo Mine Apartments

松尾鉱山 — The Ruins in the Clouds
📍 Hachimantai, Iwate Prefecture

At 1,000 meters elevation in northern Honshu, massive concrete apartment blocks emerge from the fog like something from a post-apocalyptic film. Once home to 15,000 miners and their families, this sulfur mining town was called "a paradise above the clouds."

When the mine closed in 1969, the entire community vanished. The apartment buildings — some of the first reinforced concrete housing in Japan — remain standing, slowly being consumed by mountain weather. In winter, they're buried in snow. In fog, they appear and disappear like ghosts.

Visiting

Visible from the road to Hachimantai. The buildings are fenced off and structurally dangerous — view from the outside. Best combined with a trip to Hachimantai hot springs. Autumn foliage season makes the contrast stunning.

冥界 · The Other Side

Supernatural

Where the living world meets the dead. Japan takes this literally.

Aomori Prefecture

Mount Osore (Osorezan)

恐山 — The Mountain of Dread
📍 Shimokita Peninsula, Aomori (northernmost Honshu)

One of Japan's three most sacred places — and a literal gateway to the afterlife. A Buddhist temple sits inside the caldera of an active volcano. The landscape is hellish: sulfurous steam rises from barren yellow earth, the stench of rotten eggs fills the air, and a pristine turquoise lake sits in the middle of it all.

The name means "Mountain of Dread" (from the Ainu word for the area, later matched to the Japanese word osore — fear). Founded in 862 CE. Blind spirit mediums called itako gather here during summer festivals to channel the dead. Visitors leave pinwheels and toys for deceased children.

Visiting

Open May-October only (snow closes it in winter). Entry ¥500. The Bodai-ji temple has free onsen baths fed by volcanic springs. The Itako Taisai festival (July) is when mediums communicate with the dead. Remote — nearest city is Mutsu. Worth the journey.

Iwate Prefecture

Tōno — Valley of the Kappa

遠野 — Where Folklore Is Real
📍 Tōno City, Iwate Prefecture

A rural valley where Japanese folklore isn't stories — it's daily life. This is the home of Tōno Monogatari (1910), the most important collection of Japanese folk tales ever published. The locals still leave cucumbers at the river for kappa (water demons).

The valley is dotted with shrines to oshira-sama (mulberry stick deities wrapped in cloth), zashiki-warashi (child ghosts that haunt houses and bring good fortune), and the kappa pool where the water creatures supposedly live. It's not played up for tourists — this is genuine, living folklore.

Visiting

Rent a bicycle and explore the valley. Visit Kappa-buchi pool, the Tōno Folklore Museum, and Denshōen garden. Stay at a traditional farmhouse (magariya). The Tōno Matsuri festival in September brings the folklore to life. 2-3 hours from Morioka by train.

Before You Go

⚠️

Respect the Sites

Many of these places are sacred, dangerous, or both. Don't be the tourist who takes selfies at a memorial. Some locations have real dark histories involving real people.

🗣️

Language Barrier is Real

These aren't tourist hotspots. English signage is rare. Learn basic Japanese phrases or bring a translation app. Google Translate's camera mode is your friend.

🚗

You'll Need a Car

Most of these places are off the train grid. Rent a car — Japanese roads are excellent, GPS works well, and rural driving is easy. Get an International Driving Permit before you go.

📱

Get a Pocket WiFi

Cell signal is spotty in mountain areas. A pocket WiFi router (rent at the airport) keeps you connected. Download offline maps for areas like Nichitsu and Inunaki.

🏚️

Haikyo Etiquette

Japan's urban exploration scene is serious. Don't break into buildings. Don't take souvenirs. Don't post locations of hidden spots publicly. Take only photos, leave only footprints.

🌧️

Weather Matters

These places are 10x more atmospheric in fog, rain, or overcast skies. Check weather forecasts and plan accordingly. Mount Osore is only open May-October. Matsuo Mine is best in autumn.
